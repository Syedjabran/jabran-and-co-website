Our Operating Framework

The JABRAN Method™

Five phases, each with a defined question, a defined evidence base, and a defined output. Nothing moves to the next phase on opinion alone.

Phase 01

Discover

Establish what the plant believes about itself.
Management interviews, production records review, and a first structured walk of the floor. The output is a documented gap list: every point where the plant's records and the plant's reality disagree.
Phase 02

Diagnose

Measure the losses and rank them by impact.
Time studies, downtime logging, line-balance analysis, and material-flow tracing across all six assessment areas. The output is a quantified capacity-loss ledger, ranked by hours and cost — not by anecdote.
Phase 03

Design

Build the intervention around the largest verified loss.
Corrective actions are specified with owners, sequence, and expected effect — prioritised so the first move lands on the biggest constraint. The output is the 90-day action plan and management presentation.
Phase 04

Deliver

Support execution on the floor, not from a slide.
For clients who retain us through implementation: on-site support of the action plan, removal of blockers, and verification that each fix produces its expected effect before the next begins.
Phase 05

Develop

Leave behind capability, not dependence.
A KPI framework, an operational dashboard, and training for supervisors so the measurement discipline survives our departure. The audit ends; the visibility does not.
Anatomy of a Diagnosis

How a 20% drop gets found

An illustrative walkthrough of the diagnostic logic. Consider a plant whose packing department output has fallen roughly 20% over a quarter — a pattern we see in many facilities. Management's first explanation is almost always labour. The method's job is to replace that assumption with evidence.

Q1

Is the drop real, and where exactly does it sit?

Reconcile dispatch records against packing-line counts, shift by shift and SKU by SKU. A "20% department drop" is often three different problems wearing one number — one line, one shift, or one product family may account for most of it. The diagnosis narrows before it explains.

Q2

Is packing the constraint, or the victim of one?

A packing line can only pack what reaches it. Trace upstream: is the line starved by filling, by material availability, by changeover scheduling? If operators are standing idle waiting for product, the loss lives upstream and every hour spent "fixing packing" is wasted.

Q3

If it is packing — is the loss availability, rate, or quality?

Time-stamped observation over full shifts separates the three: machine stoppages and changeovers (availability), running below rated speed and micro-stops (performance), and rejects or rework consuming finished output (quality). Each has a different owner and a different fix; conflating them guarantees the wrong intervention.

Q4

What changed when the drop began?

A step-change has a cause with a date: a new SKU with a slower pack format, a maintenance event, a staffing rotation, a material or supplier switch. Correlating the timeline of the drop against the plant's change log usually shortens the search dramatically.

Q5

What is the ranked recovery sequence?

Only now is a recommendation legitimate. The verified losses are ranked by recoverable hours, each with an owner, an action, and an expected effect — the raw material of the 90-day plan. The discipline is refusing to prescribe in week one what can only be known by week three.

What We Measure

The capacity-loss ledger

Every audit produces this ledger — the plant's losses, named, measured, and ranked. It is the difference between knowing the plant is underperforming and knowing why.

Loss Category How It Is Measured Evidence Artifact
Availability loss Time-stamped stoppage log across full shifts — breakdowns, changeovers, waiting, starvation Downtime Pareto, ranked by hours per cause
Performance loss Observed cycle rate vs. rated capacity per machine and station; micro-stop frequency counts Rate-gap table per line, with pacing station identified
Quality loss Reject, rework, and scrap tracking at point of occurrence — not point of discovery Defect-origin map with cost-of-poor-quality estimate
Flow loss Material travel distance and handling counts from raw store to dispatch; search-time sampling Spaghetti diagram + handling ledger
Maintenance loss Planned vs. forced maintenance ratio; repeat-failure analysis by asset Asset criticality register with failure history
Planning loss Schedule adherence tracking; changeover sequence analysis; handoff audit between planning and floor Adherence report with root-cause tags per deviation
